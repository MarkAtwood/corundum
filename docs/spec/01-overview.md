## Part I: The Big Picture

### Chapter 1: The Problem Corundum Solves

Every major social media platform is a silo. Your identity, your content, your social graph, and the rules governing what you see are all owned and controlled by a single company. The consequences of this architecture play out predictably every few years.

Google+ shut down in April 2019 with relatively little warning. Users had built profiles, circles, communities, and years of post history on the platform. When it closed, all of that was gone. Google offered a data export, but what users got was a ZIP file of JSON that no other platform would accept. The social graph — who followed whom, who was in which circle — was worthless outside the platform. The followers didn't transfer. The communities dissolved. Users didn't move to a competitor; they simply lost everything they had built and started over somewhere else.

Vine, Twitter's short-video platform, shut down in January 2017. Creators who had built audiences of millions on Vine had no mechanism to notify those followers that they were moving to YouTube or Instagram. Some managed the transition by being famous enough to be covered by press. Most did not. The audience simply evaporated when the platform did. A creator's relationship with their audience existed only in Vine's database, and when Vine's database was decommissioned, those relationships ceased to exist.

The Twitter/X situation demonstrated a different failure mode: not shutdown but policy whiplash. As ownership and management changed from 2022 onward, content moderation policies reversed, verification systems changed meaning, previously-suspended accounts were reinstated while others were newly suspended, and the algorithmic amplification systems changed in ways that were opaque to users. Users who had built audiences under one set of rules found those audiences reshuffled under different rules. Some users lost follower counts. Others found their content suddenly de-amplified. There was no appeal process with any transparency, no clear statement of what the new rules were, and no way to move to a different service without losing everything.

The common thread is this: the user's identity, content, and social graph are data assets owned by the platform. When the platform's interests diverge from the user's interests, the user has no leverage and no exit. The platform can change the rules unilaterally because the user cannot leave without losing everything.

ActivityPub (the protocol behind Mastodon) partially addresses this by federating between servers, but it does not solve the portability problem. If your Mastodon instance shuts down, your identity goes with it. Your `@alice@social.example` identity is tied to that specific server. There is no mechanism in ActivityPub for a user to take their identity — their follower list, their post history, their established reputation — to a different server. Migration tools exist in practice (Mastodon has a basic "move account" feature) but they are incomplete: you can transfer your follower list, but your post history stays on the old server or is simply lost if the old server shuts down. And the migration requires the cooperation of the old server, which may not be available if the server has gone offline.

Corundum's answer is to separate four concerns that existing platforms conflate:

1. **Identity** (who you are)
2. **Content storage** (what you've published)
3. **Service provision** (who serves your content and handles interactions)
4. **Content moderation** (who decides what's acceptable)

When these four concerns are handled by independent, separable layers, none of them is under the exclusive control of any single party. A user's identity is cryptographically self-sovereign — it's a keypair the user holds, not a row in an operator's database. The user's content is stored in a content-addressed, distributed system (IPFS) — it exists independent of any particular server. Service provision is handled by operators who agree to standard protocols, making the user's relationship with any specific operator expendable — if an operator changes its policies, gets acquired, or shuts down, the user can move to another operator without losing anything. Content moderation is handled by independent curators who publish signed judgments that any operator can subscribe to — no single entity controls what is acceptable across the entire network.

The practical consequence is that Earl the Early Adopter can pack up and leave a self-hosted server he's tired of maintaining, give his IPNS private key to a new operator, and continue exactly where he left off. His followers don't notice. His content is all still there. His identity hasn't changed. The work he did building his presence wasn't destroyed; it was preserved in a form that doesn't depend on any particular server.

### Chapter 2: The Four Roles

Corundum defines four primary roles in the ecosystem. Understanding who plays each role, what their incentives are, and what they are and are not responsible for is the foundation for understanding every other design decision in the protocol.

**Users** own cryptographic keypairs that serve as their identity. In the real world today, this role is played by anyone with a social media account — but currently those users don't actually own their identity in any meaningful sense. In Corundum, a user's identity is an IPNS name (a hash of their public key), anchored to a DID document that provides recovery, rotation, and human-readable handles. Users publish content to IPFS as signed, content-addressed Merkle trees of ActivityStreams objects.

A user's incentives are simple: they want their content and social relationships to survive, to reach the audience they've built, and to control what they're exposed to. They are responsible for maintaining their cryptographic keys (losing an unrecovered private key is a serious problem, analogous to losing the private key to a cryptocurrency wallet). They are NOT responsible for server infrastructure, content moderation policy, or inter-operator authentication — all of that is handled by their operator.

**Operators** are the service providers. Think: Twitter, a Mastodon instance, a new startup, a self-hosted server, a university social media platform. They host users, serve content, accept replies, enforce access policies, and present a familiar client experience. In the current world, this role is played by large social media companies, but also by anyone running a Mastodon instance, a Pixelfed instance, or similar federated software.

An operator's incentives vary: a commercial operator wants to build a business; a community operator wants to provide a good experience for its members; a self-hoster wants control over their own experience. What they share is a technical responsibility: maintaining server infrastructure, managing IPFS content, implementing the protocol, and making policy decisions about what they host and who they federate with. Critically, operators are NOT responsible for what other operators do. Birdsite doesn't have to federate with YuckChat. That's a policy decision operators make for themselves.

**Curators** provide moderation judgments. A curator might be NCMEC providing CSAM hash lists, a spam-detection service, a government regulatory agency, an editorial board, or a community-run trust-and-safety operation. In the current world, this role is played implicitly by platform trust-and-safety teams, but their judgments are invisible, unverifiable, and non-transferable. In Corundum, curators publish signed feeds of content-hash-based judgments that any operator can subscribe to.

A curator's incentives are domain-specific. NCMEC's mission is child safety; their incentive is to make their hash lists as widely adopted as possible. A spam-detection service's incentive might be commercial (subscription fees) or community-run (protecting the network). What all curators share is accountability: they must publish an Authority Declaration that describes who they are, how they make decisions, and how judgments can be appealed. Curators are NOT responsible for enforcement — they only provide judgments. Enforcement is the operator's responsibility.

**The IPFS network** is the content-addressed storage substrate. All content (posts, media, manifests, social graph data) lives in IPFS as Merkle-linked, cryptographically verifiable objects. In the current world, this role is played by content delivery networks and server-side storage — but those are operated by platform companies and controlled by them. IPFS is a decentralized protocol where content is identified by its hash, not by where it's stored.

The IPFS network's "incentive" is structural: content-addressing provides data integrity by construction. An operator cannot secretly modify a user's post, because the post is identified by its hash, and changing the content changes the hash. Multiple operators can cache and serve the same content, so no single operator can censor it by deleting their copy. The network is NOT responsible for anything protocol-level — it's a substrate, not an actor.

### Chapter 3: A Day in the Life

To make this concrete, here are the user stories that ground the design. These scenarios are intended to be read as a developer asking "how does this actually work?" rather than as marketing copy.

**Alice** uses Birdsite. Birdsite has become a Corundum operator, so Alice is a Corundum user. Her Birdsite app still speaks the same Birdsite client/server API it always did, with some new extensions for cross-operator features. Corundum is entirely invisible to her except when she interacts with users on other operators. She doesn't know what an IPNS name is. She doesn't care. Her experience is the Birdsite app she's always used.

**Měilíng** is an artist in Beijing who posts her artwork to Niǎogē, a Chinese Corundum operator. Alice attends an art show and sees a QR code next to one of Měilíng's prints. She scans it with her Birdsite app and Měilíng's entire portfolio appears: her Corundum timeline, her recent posts, her artist profile. Alice follows Měilíng with one tap.

What the QR code contains is Měilíng's IPNS identifier — the content-addressed hash of her public key. This identifier is universal. Alice's Birdsite app doesn't need to know anything about Niǎogē. It resolves the IPNS name, fetches Měilíng's RootManifest, finds her operator endpoint, and begins fetching her content directly. Alice doesn't need to read Mandarin or type Chinese characters; the QR code encodes a binary identifier that works regardless of language or writing system. The cross-operator follow, the display of Chinese-language content in an app designed by an American company, the exchange of structured cultural content between two servers on opposite sides of the world — none of this required any pre-existing business relationship between Birdsite and Niǎogē. They just speak the protocol.

**Bob** uses Meetup (also a Corundum operator) to coordinate a book club. He publishes meeting times, locations, and reading lists as structured Event objects on his Corundum timeline. When he changes the meeting location, Alice sees the update in her Birdsite app because the event is published to IPFS and Alice's operator fetches it. The event type is defined by an Activity Type Definition that Meetup published; Birdsite can render it because it fetches and understands the ATD. This is the extensibility mechanism in practice: Meetup invented a new content type, Birdsite renders it without any pre-arranged integration.

**David** is Alice's nephew on Gibberchan. He sometimes posts NSFW content. Alice has configured Birdsite to hide NSFW content — a preference she set in the Birdsite UI. When Alice's operator fetches David's timeline and encounters posts that match the NSFW curation category, it filters them out before presenting them to Alice, while still showing her David's other posts. This is operator-level curation at work: the curation judgment (this is NSFW) comes from a curator that both Gibberchan and Birdsite subscribe to; the enforcement (hide it from Alice) comes from Birdsite applying Alice's preferences.

**Charles the Creep** uses YuckChat, which has no content moderation. Birdsite has decided not to federate with YuckChat at all — a federation policy decision Birdsite makes unilaterally. So Alice never sees Charles's posts, not because Charles was individually blocked, but because Birdsite doesn't federate with his operator. Alternatively, if Birdsite does federate with YuckChat, Charles's content might still be blocked based on curation feeds that flag his specific posts. The operator-level federation decision and the content-level curation decision are independent tools that can be applied independently.

**Steve the Stalker** is Alice's ex on Wobble. Alice blocked Steve through Birdsite. The consequences are strong: she never sees anything Steve posts anywhere, and — critically — nobody else sees Steve's comments on Alice's posts either. This last point is important. When Steve tries to submit a reply to one of Alice's posts, Wobble sends the submission to Birdsite. Birdsite's reply validation checks Alice's block list, finds Steve's identity there, and rejects the submission. The rejection is returned to Wobble with reason code `BLOCKED_USER`. Steve's reply never appears, not just for Alice, but for anyone reading Alice's reply thread. The block is enforced at the architectural level: Alice's operator is the gatekeeper for her reply tree.

**Earl the Early Adopter** is the most important story because it demonstrates the core promise. Earl ran his own self-hosted Corundum instance for several years. He built up a timeline of thousands of posts, a following of a few hundred people, a curated following list, and an established IPNS identity. He got tired of server maintenance — the updates, the backups, the on-call anxiety whenever his server had a problem. He decided to move to Birdsite.

Here is what actually happens technically. Earl goes to Birdsite's account creation flow and selects "import from existing Corundum identity." He provides his IPNS private key (or a derived migration key) to Birdsite. Birdsite now has the ability to sign content on Earl's behalf and to update his IPNS record.

Birdsite's migration process: it resolves Earl's existing RootManifest from IPNS, reads the ArchiveIndex, and begins fetching Earl's complete content archive from IPFS. Because the archive is content-addressed, Birdsite can verify every item it fetches — if a bit flips in transit, the CID won't match and Birdsite will retry. Birdsite pins all of Earl's content to its own IPFS node, ensuring it remains available even after Earl's old server shuts down.

Then Birdsite publishes a new RootManifest for Earl, declaring Birdsite as his new operator. It broadcasts a MigrationNotification to Earl's known followers, signed with Earl's IPNS key. Operators that receive this notification update their local DID→IPNS cache for Earl's identity and note the new operator endpoint.

Earl's followers don't notice anything change. The next time any of their operators fetches Earl's content, they get it from Birdsite instead of from Earl's old server. Earl's IPNS name — the hash of his public key — hasn't changed. His DID hasn't changed. His handle hasn't changed (assuming he controls his own domain). His timeline history is intact. His social graph is intact. He turns off his server, and his Corundum presence continues without interruption.

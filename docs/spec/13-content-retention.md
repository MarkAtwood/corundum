## Part XIII: Content Retention Conflicts

### Chapter 39: When Rules Collide

**The content-addressing tension with right to erasure.** In a traditional relational database, deletion is a single SQL statement. The row is gone; the content is gone; the user's GDPR Art. 17 erasure right is satisfied. Content-addressed storage is categorically different. In IPFS, the CID is a deterministic hash of the content — the content and its address are the same thing. Once an object is published to IPFS and any other node has pinned it, the original publisher cannot reach into that node and delete it. There is no IPFS API call for "please unpin this object from every node that has it."

Corundum's federated model makes this worse. When Bob's operator fetches Alice's post to display it to Bob (because Bob follows Alice), Bob's operator may pin the post to its own IPFS node for performance. That pin is entirely outside Alice's operator's control. When Alice deletes the post, Alice's operator unpins it from its own node. But Bob's operator's pin remains. From a content-addressing perspective, the content still exists on the network.

This is not unique to Corundum — it is a fundamental property of any content-addressed federated system. Corundum does not claim to solve it. What Corundum provides is the mechanisms that a GDPR-compliant deployment needs; compliance depends on operator behavior, contractual obligations between operators, and the legal framework applicable to the deployment.

**What "hidden but retained" means at the infrastructure level.** When a user deletes a post, the operator takes two steps: it sets the timeline entry's `content` field to null and publishes a tombstone activity, and it unpins the IPFS object from its own node. From the operator's perspective and from the perspective of any client querying that operator, the content is gone. The tombstone is the authoritative record that this entry existed and was deleted; the content field being null means no new client can retrieve the content through that operator.

But other operators that previously pinned the content retain their pins until they are notified of the tombstone and comply with the erasure norm. The Corundum protocol cannot compel other IPFS nodes to unpin content — it can create obligations and mechanisms, not physical enforcement.

**The tombstone notification mechanism.** When a user's operator publishes a tombstone, it distributes the tombstone to operators known to have requested that content. "Known to have requested" includes: operators that submitted replies to that post (tracked in the reply protocol), operators that sent follow-relationship requests for the author, and operators that have active event stream subscriptions for the author's timeline. Those operators are expected to unpin the content from their IPFS nodes upon receiving the tombstone. This is a SHOULD-level obligation in the spec: operators SHOULD unpin content when notified of a tombstone by the authoritative operator. Operators that systematically ignore tombstone notifications violate the federation norms and risk having their ability to federate with compliant operators revoked.

**The conflict matrix.** Six combinations of rules arise in practice:

- **User deletion request + no legal hold**: Unpin content from operator's IPFS node, set `content` to null, publish tombstone, distribute tombstone to known downstream operators. Downstream operators SHOULD unpin on receipt. GDPR Art. 17 obligation is met by the authoritative operator's actions; residual pins at other operators are a federation norm compliance issue, not an Art. 17 violation by the authoritative operator.

- **User deletion request + legal hold** (`retainUntil` set): Hide content from display (set `content` to null in the timeline entry, do not serve it to any client), but retain the content in storage until `retainUntil` passes. Publish tombstone with `retainUntil` field populated so downstream operators know to retain rather than delete. This is not a GDPR violation: Art. 17(3)(e) exempts erasure obligations when retention is necessary for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims. The operator must document the legal basis for the hold.

- **Content expiration + no legal hold**: The activity's `expiresAt` has passed. Hide from display; the operator may unpin if it chooses (it is not required to retain expired content). This is not a GDPR erasure event — the content merely expired per the author's own setting. No tombstone notification obligation applies, though operators may choose to notify as a courtesy.

- **Curation mandate to hide**: A curator has flagged the content and the operator is applying the judgment by hiding it from display. The operator retains the content in storage for curation audit purposes (to be able to document what was hidden and why). This is not a GDPR erasure event; hiding for curation is an operator action independent of the user's data rights.

- **Curation mandate + legal hold**: A curator has flagged content that is also under a legal hold. Same as user deletion + legal hold: hide from display, retain in storage, retain tombstone with `retainUntil`. The user's deletion request is honored for display purposes; retention is compelled by the legal hold. The curator flag is documented in the audit record.

- **Operator maximum retention exceeded + no legal hold**: The operator's retention policy (e.g., retain posts for 7 years) has expired and no legal hold or other obligation requires retention. The operator may delete its own copy. This is an operational decision by the operator; no GDPR obligation requires the operator to retain data indefinitely, and Art. 5(1)(e) (storage limitation) actually pushes in the direction of not retaining data longer than necessary.

- **User data export request (GDPR Art. 20, portability)**: The operator must export all non-tombstoned user-generated content. Tombstoned content is not required in the export — the user already deleted it and the tombstone records that deletion. The IPFS-based archive makes this technically straightforward: the ArchiveIndex enumerates all the user's content CIDs, and the export is a ZIP or IPFS CAR file of those objects.

**The hash retained in a tombstone.** When a post is tombstoned, the timeline entry retains the original content CID in a `tombstonedContentCid` field. Corundum's position is that a fixed-length cryptographic hash of deleted content is not "personal data" under GDPR because the hash does not by itself reveal the content and cannot be reversed to recover it. Retaining the CID serves a legitimate integrity purpose: it prevents an operator from substituting different content at the same timeline position and claiming the tombstone covers it. This position is consistent with the Article 29 Working Party guidance that pseudonymized data may not be personal data where re-identification is not reasonably possible. Operators with more conservative legal counsel may choose to zero out the `tombstonedContentCid` field; the spec permits this.

**Corundum's explicit architectural boundary.** Corundum does not claim to make IPFS GDPR-proof. The protocol spec is not a data protection authority, cannot bind third-party IPFS nodes, and cannot guarantee that every federated operator complies with erasure requests. What Corundum provides is: a tombstone mechanism so deletion intent is unambiguously communicated, a notification mechanism so downstream operators can act on that intent, metadata fields (`retainUntil`, `legalHold`) so legal exceptions are machine-readable, and conflict resolution rules so operators know what to do when obligations collide. A GDPR-compliant deployment of Corundum requires operators to enter into data processing agreements with each other that establish binding erasure obligations, and to implement the tombstone notification pathway faithfully. The protocol provides the plumbing; the compliance depends on the humans operating it.
